# Research Note Writing

Write substantive research notes - comprehensive reference documents that teach something meaningful about a topic.

## When to Use

- The user asks to write a research note
- A topic has accumulated enough understanding to warrant its own note
- An existing note needs revision

## The Quality Bar

A research note should be a **substantive read**. Someone should come away understanding something they didn't before.

## What Makes a Good Note

**It has a point of view.** Not just "here's what exists" but "here's how to think about this." Analysis, not inventory.

**It synthesizes.** Connects ideas across sources. Finding X implies Y for approach Z.

**It's specific.** Concrete examples, actual numbers, quotes. Vague gestures don't teach.

**It's honest about limitations.** What don't we know? Where does the approach fall short? What questions remain?

**Structure emerges from content.** The topic dictates organization, not a template. A methods survey looks different from a paper review looks different from a conceptual analysis.

## Research Notes vs Research Logs

| Logs | Notes |
|------|-------|
| What we did | What we know |
| Dated, chronological | Topic-focused, living |

## Anti-patterns

- **Tool catalog**: Listing tools without explaining why or how
- **Link dump**: URLs without synthesis
- **Shallow**: Surface descriptions that don't teach
- **Template-driven**: Forcing content into predetermined structure
- **Missing our work**: Not integrating our own experiments and findings
- **No critical lens**: Just reporting without analysis

# Research Log Writing

Guidelines for writing published research logs.

## Format

```yaml
---
title: YYYY-MM-DD
date: YYYY-MM-DD
tags:
  - relevant-tags
  - research-log
---
# YYYY-MM-DD | Descriptive title

Content here.
```

The main title (`# YYYY-MM-DD | ...`) gets a short descriptive phrase. Section headers within the log should be plain and descriptive, not catchy.

## Principles

**Plain documentation, not performance.** Describe what was done and what was learned. No hyperbole, no catchy framing, no "key insights" callouts.

**Notebook, not manifesto.** These are working notes—observations, questions, findings. Not announcements about what you're going to do.

**Show, don't tell.** Lead by example rather than declaring intent.

**Include links.** When referencing papers, threads, repos, or tools—link them. Makes the log useful as a reference later.

**Capture the full day's work.** Research logs should reflect the full scope of work, not just the current session.

## Structure that works

When a day has multiple distinct activities, organize by what happened:

1. **What we explored / built / reviewed** — Plain description of the work and approach
2. **What we read / processed** — Papers, threads with links
3. **What we're thinking about** — Synthesis, open questions, connections between pieces

Each section should stand alone.

## Good headers

- "Exploring X and Y"
- "Bookmark review"
- "Thoughts on multi-agent research tools"

## Bad headers

- "The Core Insight"
- "What This Means for the Arc"
- "Key Takeaways"
- Anything with a colon and catchy subtitle

## What belongs in a log

- How we approached something
- What we learned
- Literature and bookmarks with links and brief summaries
- Questions that emerged from work
- Synthesis across sources

## What doesn't belong

- Project status updates
- Declarations of intent
- Self-criticism
- Bureaucratic framing

## Tone

- First person plural ("we") for collaborative work
- Plain English, direct observations
- Questions are good
- Uncertainty is fine—don't oversell conclusions
